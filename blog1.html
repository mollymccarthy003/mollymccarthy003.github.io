The Process Behind My Owl Linocut

Published on December 7, 2025

Linocut printing has been one of my favorite creative outlets for about 3 years now. My owl linocut is one of my favorites I have made, and I haven’t attempted a design as detailed since! I have always loved owls, specifically snowy and barn owls. I wanted to create a combination of the two that had a whimsical border with the moon phases. It was an elaborate design that I am so excited to share the process with you!



At its core, linocut is simple. Start by carve a design into the linoleum block, roll on ink, press it onto a surface and reveal a handmade print. It is a long process made for intentional decisions at every step. Most of my linocuts start as a simple sketch on some sort of transfer paper. Then I transfer it onto the linoleum block and begin to carve it. At this point in the process I had not sketched the border.



Tiny V-gouges were used for feather lines, while large U-gouges cleared the negative space around the border. I used a harder linoleum, which made carving more difficult and I had to be more precise. If you are a beginner I suggest using thicker linoleum blocks instead of the thin hard pieces. I worked slowly, frequently stopping to brush away debris and to check the depth of the cut. This owl linocut took 8 hours from sketch to fully carved, including the flower moon phase border.



The next step was inking. For a detailed print like this, rolling on a smooth layer of ink is essential. Too much ink fills the carved lines, but too little prints unevenly and your details aren’t as powerful. Each print will have slight variations, giving it its own personality. Seeing the finished owl was so satisfying.


The process of creating this owl reminded me why linocut is so fun:

  • It rewards patience
  • It transformed simple tools into bold artwork
  • It results in prints that feel alive
  • It has so much variation, here are some of the same print but on a tote bag!


My tips for a long creative session:

  • Take breaks
  • Hydrate
  • Watch something, I watched RuPaul's Drag Race Season 2!

Linocut teaches you to slow down and trust the process. By the end of eight hours, I felt not only tired but deeply proud! Art created with time and intention has a different type of presence. I made this lino over 2 years ago, but it still is one of my all-time favorites.
